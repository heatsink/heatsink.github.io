Autonomous Golf Cart            >

Autonomous Golf Cart

TL;DR: collision detection & navigation, ROS, home-made PCB. Python, lidar, and other inferior sensors (just kidding, they're all essential).

The autonomous golf cart is one of three flagship projects that the Cal Poly Robotics team is currently dedicated to. I'm part of what we affectionally call the IGVC team, even though we don't (yet) compete in the Intelligent Ground Vehicle Competition. But we do challenge and compete against ourselves to do some quality engineering! Or something like that...

Our team is interdisciplinary, comprising of electrical, mechanical, and computer engineers. We also have a geologist who happens to know a lot about engineering. This allows us to claim diversity.

Within the IGVC team, I specialize in collision detection & navigation. We use a SICK 2D Lidar, which has the the dual purpose of detecting solid objects in a 270 degree field of view as well as be the basis for many sick puns. Currently, our team is experimenting with ROS's navigation stack for proof of concept as well as inspiration for our own in-house navigation stack. The design and programming of this milestone will take place during Fall 2016.

This is a large-scale long term project so it is entirely possible that it will continue until graduation. Therefore, it is important to have a solid definition for project success. Otherwise, we'll never end up pursing another project! The IGVC team will declare the project successful when the golf cart can drive to and from the nearest Woodstock's Pizza and return with a delicious payload. Autonomously. Worry not, the pizza will be in good hands.

Even though I focus on software within the Robotics team, I still interact with hardware on a regular basis. This PCB was designed by my current team members before I joined the club, and I will design a module to add onto the board for a speedcontroller during Spring 2016.

Ninja Fighter                                >

Ninja Fighter

TL;DR: Java. Object Oriented Programming. Game design. GitHub.


Ninja Fighter is a top-down action RPG written in Java using Greenfoot. It was a collaborative effort, made with four other members during a course in game design. Ninja Fighter also happens to share the same name with many other ninja games, but this was unintentional. We signed some forms supposedly granting immunity from copyright claims under the presumption that we wouldn't try to make money from the project. These university powers were pretty great, as it allowed us to focus our time on programming game mechanics.

Coincidentally, our animations, sound effects, and background music are on point. Thanks to the work of many fine artists working at Blizzard, Nexon, and many other game companies. Don't worry, we gave credit through comments in our source code. And again, strictly educational purposes. No profit. In the end, Ninja Fighter ended up looking like a pretty cool ninja game with classic gameplay mechanics with a cheesy storyline. As is tradition.


The storyline of Ninja Fighter is conveyed (badly) through cinematics. In the beginning, a lone boy picking berries when "The Darkness" suddenly envelops his village. A mysterious master rescues the boy, who takes him to his cave to train him to become a ninja. The player experiences this training, and becomes a ninja that fights to save his village from the darkness. (This was the best storyline that the combined effort of five Cal Poly computer and software engineers could come up with. I hope this doesn't diminish the reputation of the university).



The player follows the master through a corridor, but the master is nowhere to be seen. In fact, the master is never seen again.


As much fun I poke at this past project I've worked on, I think Ninja Fighter actually does a pretty good job of introducing players to gameplay mechanics. We didn't have to implement those lazy chatboxes that tell players to "Press W to move! :D" (this is far too common these days). During the training levels, the boy learns how to use a sword by walking on a sword rack (very realistic). The same mechanic is in place where he walks on a table covered with shurikens to learn how to throw them. Hopefully, players learn that walking on objects do good things. Later, as ninjas, players walk over barrels to replenish their shurikens, apples to regenerate health, and eggs to replenish their power. All good things.


The game is split into 3 acts with different themes (possibly inspired by Blizzard's Diablo). We have a grasslands, haunted forest, and fire (Inferno!) act. Each act has monsters that somewhat correspond to the theme along with different game mechanics. At the end of each act is a bossfight, where players earn a new ability upon completion of the act. By the time players reach the fire levels, they know how to dash as well as be temporarily invulnerable.


What's an offline RPG without a cheat code? Pressing 1, 3, 5, and 7 activated "Haung's Mode", named after Dr. Haungs (our game design professor). It made the player really overpowered, and would feature a badly photoshopped Captain "Haungs" America. It was really funny at the time.


Remember that part where the master training the boy to become a ninja mysteriously disappears? Turns out, the master had been controlling the darkness the entire time. Dude. Intense, right? Who would've thought?

When I look back on this project, I tend to poke a lot of fun at the code I wrote and the game we produced. The phenomenon of programmers cringing over old code they wrote is not lost on me. I think this game was still worth documenting, as it serves as a reminder of constant improvement in programming practices. And it truly isn't that bad! Ninja Fighter was really popular with my two 7 and 8 year old cousins. They wouldn't stop asking to play it...

Although Ninja Fighter could certainly be improved, I think the goal of the project was certainly achieved. Programming individually has its challenges, but the logistics of coding with others was a whole new beast to get used to. Also, we used Git for version control, and this was where I was forced to learn how to resolve merge conflicts. They gave me nightmares at the time... However, I am happy to report that these are no longer personal issues! I becaem a fan of Git. Some people are thankful for the turky on the table during Thanksgiving. I'm thankful for Git. Okay, maybe not that far.
